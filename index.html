NOMAD DAYS

An Appalachian Trail Photo Journal

The Appalachian Trail is a blaze-marked footpath that extends over 2000 miles along the ridges, hills, and highlands that comprise the Appalachian Mountain range in the eastern portion of the United States. The A. T., as it is known among hikers, is really a network of interconnected trails, roads, sidewalks, bridges, and even a river that one must ferry across by canoe. Avid hikers and backpackers flock to the A. T., and many try to complete the entire trail in one epic push known as a “thru-hike”. In early March, 2017 my wife, Charle, our dog, Bridger, and I set out on an attempted Thru-Hike of the A. T.

The A. T. passes through 14 states:

  • Georgia
  • North Carolina
  • Tennessee
  • Virginia
  • West Virginia
  • Maryland
  • Pennsylvania
  • New Jersey
  • New York
  • Connecticut
  • Massachusetts
  • Vermont
  • New Hampshire
  • Maine

GEORGIA

Springer Mountain in Georgia is the southern terminus of the A. T., and hikers attempting a thru-hike beginning in Georgia headed north are referred to as northbounders, or NOBOs.. Alternatively, hikers beginning their hike in the north headed south are called SOBOs. Our journey began in the south, thus we were NOBOs.

There are 76 miles of A. T. in Georgia. The vast majority of NOBO hikers starting a thru-hike begin here in early spring. March is the busiest time on the trail in GA. Many think of hiking the Appalachian Trail as being an exercise in solitude, and it certainly can be, but at any given time there are between 50 and 100 fellow thru-hikers within a few miles of you. This is known as a “bubble”. You get to know the hikers in your bubble pretty well. The A. T. has a justified reputation among long trails as having a really strong community. The Georgia section really gives a fledgling thru -hiker a taste of that community.

NORTH CAROLINA

The weather swings dramatically on the A. T. in general, but March is another beast altogether. Down in Georgia we enjoyed sunny, 70 degree days, and we endured snow, ice, and sub 0 temps. Crossing into North Carolina was a bitter- sweet victory. Crossing state lines while on the A. T. usually puts wind in your sails. The day we crossed into NC was our hardest day yet. The cold had us frozen to the bone, and the wind bore right through our clothing. The trail was covered all over in icy, crusty snow that made the going slow. We encountered some of the biggest up-hill climbs on the day we passed into NC. It was a tough start, but crossing the 100 mile mark gave us resolve, and besides, all that suffering came with an equal amount of beauty.

TENNESSEE

The trail disregards the North Carolina/Tennessee border. It weaved across it so many times we lost count. The only border boost we got in this section was crossing into and out of our nation’s most visited national park; the Great Smoky Mountains National Park. The park doesn’t allow dogs on trails, so we had to board Bridger for a week while we hiked. It was odd not having our trail buddy with us, but we were able to cover more miles more quickly, and we knew he would be enjoying his little break.

VIRGINIA

On the A. T., Virginia is hands down the single state with the most miles. Over 540 miles of trail run from the border with Tennessee in the south up to West Virginia in the north. With so many miles comes so many milestones.

Our first milestone was crossing the 500 mile mark in Grayson Highlands State Park. 500 miles is a big number for a hiker. It is the number that distinguishes a “long-distance” hiker from a regular hiker. Arbitrary, I know, but it is similar to how an “ultra-marathoner” is distinguished from a marathoner.

A less happy milestone was when we sent Bridger to stay with Charle’s parents in Wisconsin. Before setting out on our thru-hike we had made plans for Bridger if he couldn’t finish the trail with us. Thru-hiking is really, really tough on the human body, and just as hard on a dog’s body. Every day hiking with Bridger we had to stay in tune with his health and needs. Most days he was great, keeping up and enjoying the trail. Other days, we could tell he was as roughed up as we were. Hiking in the Shenandoah National Park was hot, and we could tell Bridger was done. It was sad saying goodbye to our best buddy, but it was the right choice for all of us. Bridger spent the rest of the summer living the lazy dog life. For us, logistics became much simpler. We could now sit-down in a restaurant instead of having to order out. Our lodging options multiplied, and we no longer had to carry Bridger’s extra weight.

WEST VIRGINIA & MARYLAND

Passing into the mid-atlantic states means passing into more high population areas. Maryland was mostly a series of road-crossings and really easy trails. Our bellies loved the road crossings since we could usually find some good food nearby, and our legs loved the easy trails.

PENNSYLVANIA

Pennsylvania, derided as “Rocksylvania” among hikers due to its rugged boulder fields, is one of the heftier mid-atlantic states comprising 229 miles of rock strewn trail. The true mid-point of the A. T. is found within the state as well.

NEW JERSEY & NEW YORK

The mid-atlantic section of the A. T. offers less in the way of views and nature exposure, but the border crossings and amenities offered by being in high population centers make these states a great cruise section. New Jersey was hot, swampy, and filled with mosquitos.

New York is very similar to New Jersey except the trail becomes giant granite domes, and the pizza gets way better.

CONNECTICUT & MASSACHUSETTS

Connecticut was one of my favorite states. The trail was wonderful, flat and following water most of the way. The communities we passed through were filled with supportive, friendly folk. The best part of our time in the state was getting to see a couple of our best friends, Amy and Kyle, who drove all the way out from Wisconsin to hike and spend time with us. That joy was tempered by the fact that we would no longer be hiking with Tina and Molly. They got ahead of us, and we would not be able to catch up to them.

Massachusetts was much like Connecticut except the hill climbs started getting steeper. It was good preparation for what was to come in Vermont.

VERMONT

Crossing into Vermont is getting back into the wild and rugged aspects of trail life. Vermont is home to the Long Trail, a 272 mile footpath that begins at the Massachusetts border in the south and runs north all the way to the Canadian border. The A. T. joins the Long Trail for the first 100 miles. These are some of the tougher miles on the A. T. as hikers enter into the Green Mountains. The climbs are steep and long, getting up into Boreal and sub-Alpine forests. A nickname for the state is “Ver-mud” as the low sections are often feet-deep mud pits. We slowed down in Vermont not only because of the physical difficulty of thru-hiking in the state, but also because of its beauty. A normal schedule on the A. T. means hiking a certain number of miles each day to make it to a certain camping area or town at a certain time. In Vermont we would set up camp early if we found a particular area inviting enough. There are many mountain brooks in the Green Mountains, and when one is thru-hiking, cool water is so inviting!

NEW HAMPSHIRE

NOBO thru-hikers crossing into New Hampshire have gone through a lot. The Green Mountains in Vermont are training for the White Mountains in New Hampshire. Luckily, the border town, Hanover offers hikes a well deserved rest. There are a handful of “trail towns” the A. T. passes through. Each has its unique level of hospitality, amenities, and overall charm. Hanover was my favorite trail town. It was the perfect respite before climbing into the White Mountains.

The White Mountains in New Hampshire are kind of a big deal. The trail climbs true alpine peaks here where one must keep a close eye on the weather. Up above the treeline you are in an environment unsheltered from lightning, hail, fierce winds, and fog that can be absolutely blinding. The crown jewel of the range is Mount Washington, which, for decades, held the record for strongest recorded wind speeds not associated with a cyclonic storm. Many people have succumbed to the elements here. The range is beautiful, but it is equally dangerous.

MAINE

For a NOBO thru-hiker, Mount Katahdin in Maine is the ultimate goal. Crossing into the final state means you have hiked nearly 2000 miles, and have fewer than 300 to go. That final stretch is some of the toughest mileage there is. Maine is a wilderness state, and the A. T. is routed through some of the more remote areas within that state. It is a grand challenge, one that NOBOs have walked a long way to experience. Of all the miles we walked the hardest and most rewarding were in Maine.
